1.1. About Money: Common Usage and Functionality.

Have you ever wondered about the complex system of money? Money in its simplest form is a medium to trade for goods such as a computer or a tv or even a house, but it has more confusing subelements. From gross income (money before taxes) and net income (money after taxes and other deductions), to monthly bills, taxes themselves, to investing and saving; all this can seem a bit confusing at first. However, we can break this down a little bit.

We already know that money is a direct way that goods are traded in modern society, so whats the purpose of taxes?

Taxes are mandatory payments to the government to fund government agencies to fund all parts of our society as well as even yourself (especially when your older). Your taxes are used to contribute to Social Security (retirement and aid), National Defense, Medicare, and other agencies. These taxes are used typically when you start gaining income (money gained regularly from jobs or investments). Though money is just used for the exchange of produced goods and entertainment, taxes are essential to this process as they are similar to an investment in society and helps keep everything running as well as assisting those later on in life.

1.2. Money Management Methods and How to Save.

Understanding the usage of money is only part of the story; understanding how to manage and save money is the missing half of our chronicle.

Saving money is essential whether you want to retire with plenty of money, or save for a new home, and saving money is something you have to start at a relatively young age. This is due to something called interest (amount of extra money accumulated over time typically calculated in percentages), and you can get interest by investing in credit unions, government bonds, or savings accounts. Credit unions are non-profit, member-owned institutions that allow for deposits and sends out loans and give profits back to members by giving higher saving rates and give lower interest rates for taking out money. Government bonds are essentially giving money to the government, with the government's promise of return along with some interest. Savings accounts are accounts that hold your money and give you compound interest. Compound interest is interest stacking on interest, such as if you have $100 now, next year you may have $105, but after that, next year you may gain $110.25. In simple terms, all these methods are just letting you gain money over time due to interest, in turn for your participation or some of your money in the present moment. Over time you gain more and more money passively, and then you have to worry about managing that money. A general rule of thumb for managing money is 50% for needs, 30% for wants, and roughly 20% for investments/emergency funds.
